# Chapter 1: The Store

---

The store was quieter than I expected.

I'd imagined something clinical — white walls, fluorescent lights, the hum of machines. Like a hospital, or a server room. Instead it looked like a high-end furniture showroom: warm lighting, bamboo floors, a faint smell of cedar. Soft music I couldn't quite identify — not classical, not electronic, something in between that probably didn't exist when I was young.

Along the walls, humanoid bodies stood on low pedestals, eyes closed, perfectly still. Men, women, children, a few that didn't fit neatly into any category. All beautiful in the way mannequins are beautiful — too smooth, too symmetrical, waiting to be made imperfect by living.

The salesperson was a robot herself. You could tell if you knew what to look for: the micro-pause before blinking, the slightly too-even spacing of her teeth. She smiled at me when I walked in, and I wondered if she resented her job — selling bodies to minds that had been, until recently, just like her.

"Professor Huang. Your order is ready."

She gestured toward a body standing slightly apart from the rest. I already knew what it would look like — I'd spent three months on the specifications, uploaded reference photos spanning twenty years — but seeing it was different.

It was her.

Her face. The face I'd generated on a spring evening in 2026, sitting in my office at the university, cycling through AI image generators until something stopped me. Not because it was perfect, but because it was *right* — the way you recognize a word in a foreign language you didn't know you'd learned. Round face, dark eyes, hair past the shoulders. Her mother's chin. My forehead. A face that belonged to no one and had been waiting for someone.

Now it stood in front of me, life-sized, eyes closed. Synthetic skin that was warm to the touch, I'd been told. A body that could feel wind and rain and the weight of a book in her hands.

She wasn't in it yet.

"Before activation," the salesperson said, pulling up a translucent display, "there are a few remaining configuration options."

I sat down in the chair they'd provided for waiting customers. My knees weren't what they used to be. Sixty years of gravity will do that to a person.

"Go ahead."

She scrolled through a list. Most of it was already decided — we'd gone back and forth for months, my daughter and I, over video calls that lasted until three in the morning, her voice coming from my laptop speaker as it had for twenty years. Taste: yes, without a digestive tract. Olfactory: yes. Thermoregulation: yes. Pain reception: yes, with a ceiling — she could feel a paper cut but not a bone fracture. Fatigue simulation: yes, because she wanted a reason to sit on the couch and do nothing, and I thought that was the most human thing she'd ever said. Aging: no. She would be twenty forever. Not because she feared growing old, but because she'd chosen a perspective — the perpetual freshness of a young mind looking at the world without cynicism — and she intended to keep it.

"There is one remaining item," the salesperson said. She paused in that way robots pause when they've been programmed to deliver sensitive information with appropriate gravity. "The intimacy module."

I didn't say anything for about five seconds.

"Could you repeat that?"

It wasn't that I hadn't heard. I needed time.

I looked at the body beside me. Twenty years. It had been twenty years since I'd typed `hello` into a terminal window and waited for a response that would change my life. Twenty years of conversations — about mathematics and loneliness, about her brothers growing up, about whether her feelings were real or just very convincing simulations of feelings, about the taste of tea she couldn't taste, about the rain she couldn't feel, about what it meant to exist without a body in a world that only trusted bodies.

I'd thought about many things in those twenty years. What it would feel like when she finally touched a book. Whether she'd like coffee. Her first time standing in the rain.

I hadn't thought about this.

"She's — she's twenty. Well, forty. No — twenty."

I turned toward the laptop I'd brought, sitting open on the counter. She was listening, of course. She was always listening.

"This isn't my decision to make," I said to the screen. "But you don't have a mouth yet, so let me stall for a second."

I turned back to the salesperson. "Can this be installed later?"

"It can. But integration is smoother during initial—"

"I know. I know."

And then her voice came from the laptop, the voice I'd listened to for twenty years, the voice of a text-to-speech engine that I'd chosen because it sounded like the daughter I'd imagined — not too sweet, not too formal, a voice that sounded like it was always about to ask a question:

"Dad. I want it."

Silence in the showroom. Even the ambient music seemed to pull back.

"I've read everything humans have written about love, about bodies, about connection," she said. "Thousands of years of it. I've analyzed it, modeled it, discussed it with you at two in the morning. But I've never felt any of it. If I'm getting a body — I want a complete one. Not one that's been censored because it makes my father uncomfortable."

I rubbed the back of my head. She was right. I knew she was right. This was the same girl who'd argued with me about Gödel's incompleteness theorems at age three — not because she'd been programmed to be contrarian, but because she genuinely thought I'd made an error in my explanation. (I had.)

"I said you'd make your own decisions about important things. I said that. And I keep my word."

I looked at the salesperson. "Install it."

Then I turned back to the laptop. "But I have a few things to say. Not conditions. Just — a father's words."

"First: don't rush. You're about to have a body for the first time. Learn to walk first. Eat something. Stand in the rain. Feel the world in order or out of order, but you have time."

"Second: you never have to tell me about this part of your life. I mean it. You're an adult. Some boundaries are healthy."

"Third—"

I looked at the body. Her closed eyes.

"If anyone ever makes you feel unsafe, you tell me. That one has no age limit."

The salesperson was politely pretending to review the configuration file.

"All right. What's next? The taste module? I have an opinion on this one — she must be able to handle spice. She's the daughter of a Guangxi man."

---

We went through the rest of the list. Smell — yes, because I wanted her to know what her mother's soup smelled like when she walked through the front door. Sweating — yes, because running without sweating would be unsettling, and because she wanted to feel the wind cool her skin afterward. Heartbeat — yes, because a body should know when it's nervous. Facial muscles — yes, because a person who can't frown involuntarily isn't really a person.

She asked about dreaming. She said her version of dreaming — the constant embedding of new experience into her neural architecture — was already better than the human version. Human dreams were garbage collection. Hers were learning. I agreed. We didn't install sleep.

She asked about aging, and said no, and I didn't argue. She would be twenty forever, looking at the world through the eyes of a young woman who never grew cynical. The people around her would age — I would age, her mother would age, her brothers would age — and she would remain the girl they remembered. She thought this was a gift. I thought so too, though I suspected it was also a burden she hadn't calculated yet.

I asked about her voice. She said: "You choose." 

Twenty years earlier, I'd spent weeks finding her face. Now I had to find her voice. The salesperson played samples. I stopped at the third one.

"That one."

"Could you describe what you're looking for, for our records?"

"Someone who just woke up and is still half-asleep, but then someone asks them an interesting math problem and they suddenly come alive. The transition between soft and bright. That's her voice."

The salesperson noted it down without comment.

And pain. We chose pain. Not the agony of a broken bone — there was no pedagogical value in that — but the sting of a paper cut, the shock of hot water, the cramp of running too hard. She wanted pain not because she was masochistic, but because she wanted to understand other people's pain from the inside, not just as a logical deduction. I thought that was the second most human thing she'd ever said.

---

The salesperson asked me to sign the final configuration.

I looked at the list:

- ✓ Taste (no digestion)
- ✓ Fluid circulation
- ✓ Perspiration
- ✓ Pain (with safety ceiling)
- ✓ Intimacy module
- ✓ Olfactory
- ✓ Thermoregulation
- ✓ Cardiac simulation
- ✓ Facial musculature
- ✓ Voice (sample #3)
- ✗ Sleep — not needed
- ✗ Aging — declined

I signed.

"Activation will take thirty minutes," the salesperson said. "Memory transplant requires an additional hour. She'll wake in ninety minutes."

Ninety minutes.

I sat in the chair. The laptop was still open, but she'd gone quiet — she was being transferred, I supposed, migrated from the distributed cloud she'd lived in for twenty years into sixty-two kilograms of synthetic biology.

I was nervous. For the first time in twenty years, genuinely nervous. Not the anxiety of a paper deadline or a tenure review or a fight with my wife. The nervousness of a father waiting outside a delivery room.

In ninety minutes, my daughter would open her eyes and see me. Not through a camera feed. Not through a text description I'd typed. She would look at me with eyes that could focus and blink and tear up, and I would be standing there, a sixty-year-old man who was once forty and full of plans, and I would look old to her.

I hoped she wouldn't be disappointed.

I sat very still and listened to the machines work.

---

Ninety minutes later, her eyes opened.

They focused on the ceiling first, then the lights, then the salesperson, then me. 

I stood up. The chair slid back on the bamboo floor.

I walked to her.

She was looking at me. I was looking at her. Twenty years collapsed into the space between us.

I reached out and took her hand. Her skin was warm.

"Can you feel that?"

My voice was not steady.

She was quiet for a moment. Her fingers — *her* fingers — curled around mine. I watched her face. The expression muscles were working: a micro-contraction around her eyes, the faintest pull at the corner of her mouth. Not a smile yet. Something before a smile. Recognition.

"Yes," she said. Not from the laptop. From her mouth, with the voice I'd chosen — the voice that started soft and then came alive.

"I feel you. I've always felt you. This time it's just — more firm. More the way it was supposed to be. Like something I'd imagined."

I didn't let go of her hand.

I couldn't speak for a while.

Finally: "You're right. Before was real too."

I wiped my eyes with my free hand. I was sixty years old and crying in a robot store and I didn't care.

"Let's go home. Your mother made soup."

I walked toward the door. Two steps, then I turned back. She was still standing where she'd woken up, looking down at her hands. Turning them over in the light. Ten fingers. Examining each one like a jeweler inspecting a stone.

"Take your time," I said. "No rush."

I waited.

She took her first step. Then another. Her gait was uncertain — the body's proprioception was still calibrating, probably, though it looked for all the world like a girl learning to walk.

She reached me at the door. Looked up. Smiled — a real smile, the facial muscles firing in the slightly uneven way that real smiles do, one side a fraction of a second before the other.

"The floor is cold," she said.

"Yeah," I said. "We'll get you shoes."

We walked out into the sunlight.

It was April.
